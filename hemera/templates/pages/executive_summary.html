Key Findings

{{ org_name }}'s total organisational carbon footprint for the reporting period is {{ "%.1f"|format(total_co2e) }} tCO2e, with a 95% confidence interval of {{ "%.1f"|format(ci_lower) }}–{{ "%.1f"|format(ci_upper) }} tCO2e. This uncertainty range reflects the current mix of emission factor data sources — improving data quality will narrow this range in future reporting periods (see Section 8: Data Quality Improvement).

Scope 3 value chain emissions account for {{ "%.0f"|format(scope3_pct) }}% of the total footprint, which is typical for service-sector and SME organisations where purchased goods and business travel dominate. Direct emissions (Scope 1) and purchased energy (Scope 2) together represent {{ "%.0f"|format(scope1_pct + scope2_pct) }}% — these are under the organisation's direct operational control and are typically the first targets for reduction initiatives. However, the dominance of Scope 3 means that meaningful decarbonisation requires engagement with the supply chain: working with key suppliers to obtain specific emission data, switching to lower-carbon alternatives, and optimising procurement decisions.

Emission factors are sourced from the UK Government's DEFRA/DESNZ Greenhouse Gas Conversion Factors (2023, 2024) and the DEFRA supplementary EEIO spend-based factors by SIC code (2022). These are the UK's official factors for corporate carbon reporting and are updated annually by the Department for Energy Security and Net Zero. Where activity data is unavailable, Hemera applies spend-based factors from this dataset, with each estimate carrying a quantified uncertainty score.

The 95% confidence interval of {{ "%.1f"|format(ci_lower) }}–{{ "%.1f"|format(ci_upper) }} tCO2e means the true footprint is statistically likely to fall within this range. The width of the interval ({{ "%.1f"|format(ci_upper - ci_lower) }} tCO2e) reflects current reliance on spend-based emission factors rather than activity-based or supplier-specific data. For decision-making, this means reduction targets should be set against the central estimate while acknowledging that actual emissions could be meaningfully higher or lower. As data quality improves over successive reporting periods, this interval will narrow, giving greater confidence in reported figures and reduction claims.

The data quality grade of {{ data_quality_grade }} reflects the current reliance on spend-based emission factors (Level 4 in the cascade model). Sections 6–7 of this report present specific, prioritised actions to reduce emissions, and Section 9 details how data quality improvements will increase the precision of future reports. The recommended next steps are: (1) engage top suppliers for specific emission data, (2) collect activity-based measurements for energy and travel, and (3) re-assess after 12 months with improved data to track both emission reductions and uncertainty narrowing.
